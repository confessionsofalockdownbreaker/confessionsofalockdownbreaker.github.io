A no-way ticket

December 28th, 2020

For the time in-between leaving Bulgaria and finding permanent(ish) accommodation in Berlin, we’ve been grappling with the wondrous intricacies of German bureaucracy. One of the more delightful episodes involves figures out how to successfully catch the metro.

In the UK there seems to be a general perception of Germany that it is a very efficient and punctual place, and therefore the metro services must be inefficient and punctual. But not so.

Germany opts for a metro system which has no ticket barriers, unlike the London and New York metros, where barriers and transport staff stop you from skipping the fare. The barrierless German platforms ask you to buy a ticket — stamp the ticket to validate it at a separate location — before getting on the metro.

Highlight

In the UK there seems to be a general perception of Germany that it is a very efficient and punctual place, and therefore the metro services must be inefficient and punctual. But not so.

The ticket people (called “controllers” by the locals, in appropriately authoritarian undertones) are dressed in plain clothes, and can only be identified once they begin wielding ticket machines.

This leads to a constant state of paranoia, with the rarity of inspectors egging you on to risk the fine and become a criminal for your short twenty-minute journey. But then you nervously eye up different commuters with extreme suspicion as might in a John le Carré, where Russian spies are replaced by well-disguised ticket inspectors.

On one occasion, we overheard a very intense conversation between a Pakistani-looking woman and what appeared to be two burly German citizens asking very loudly if the woman could show her residency card. It wasn’t until they took out a machine to fine her that we realised they weren’t ordinary citizens.

The fine is only €60, though, and with a €2.90 single ticket it doesn’t take you long to work out how much you’d save if you only got, say, a fine every month and managed to dodge it the rest of the time.

We found out that time how frustrating, and seemingly pointless German bureaucracy could be. We since found ourselves dealing with an abundance of contradictory advice, ranging from tax issues to Covid tests.

But when we turned up, we heard no word of it. Were we a defect member of the church? We may never find out.
