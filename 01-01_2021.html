Renting in Berlin

January 1st, 2021

Another noticeable issue is living in Berlin itself, a place notoriously bad for renting. One of the reasons for this is the competition. Only around half of Germans own their own homes, the third lowest rate of home-ownership in the world behind only Hong Kong and Switzerland.

There are plenty of boring reasons why Germans tend not to own their own homes like real-estate transfer taxes being high and a lack of mortgage interest-rate tax deductions. But there are plenty of interesting reasons, too, like Germans being very averse to debt in general.

Unlike the UK, German debt isn’t automatically cancelled when someone goes bankrupt, and can even be passed on to the debtor’s children if the court decides so. Say your estranged, alcoholic, gambling-indebted father suddenly has an accident, you will need to actively disclaim inheritance of the debt to avoid it.

Highlight

Only around half of Germans own their own homes, the third lowest rate of home-ownership in the world behind only Hong Kong and Switzerland.

Does that get rid of it for good? Actually, no. Instead, the debt can just skip you in the line of succession, heading to the monetary futures of your children, who also then have to disclaim the inheritance, then leaving it to their children, and so forth.

This, apparently, is one of the reasons Germans are relatively risk averse and prefer not to take out large debts for themselves, which extends into them not wanting to take out large mortgages.

All of this makes renting hard and competitive at the best of times, let alone in the midst of a rapidly-mutating virus. But after several months of hard work, and a fair amount of lost cash, we’d managed to get Anmeldung (German for registration) – a legal signifier in Germany which acts as a kind of gatekeeping prelude to full residency.

It is illegal to live in the country without Anmeldung. But there’s a catch. You need to find someone to register you, which is a legal requirement for a landlord in Germany but one which involves a lot of paperwork. Such landlords are less willing to go through such effort for short-term lets, but opting for a long-term let locks us into a city with one of the most dramatic Covid case spikes in Europe. It also typically costs more money.

In terms of renting a house and living in Berlin, none of this has so far worked in our favour. We leave out accommodation on January 7th, and after that we have six weeks until Anmeldung runs out.

Our Brexit appointment is sometime between now and June – without any notification in advance. If we end up at the appointment without Anmeldung, we might not be allowed to stay in Germany at all. So after finding a one-month apartment on the steeper side of the budget, we are once again on the hunt in the Berlin renting market, searching for another ever-more elusive anmeldung.

The deadline is in June 2021, or at least sometime before then, when we have a Brexit meeting to decide whether or not we will be allowed to stay in Germany. Whether we find an apartment with a reasonable landlord in the meantime will be the deciding factor.
